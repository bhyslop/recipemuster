# Three-heat plan-integrity audit — process retrospective

*Written 2026-06-25, immediately after running the audit directed by `memo-20260625-three-heat-integrity-audit-directive.md` and applying its findings. This memo records the PROCESS and why it earned confidence — it is provenance, not authority. The durable outputs (the corrected dockets and paddocks) live in the gallops, not here; this memo never becomes a second source for them.*

## What happened, in one paragraph
A fresh chat re-derived the integrity of the ₣Bf / ₣Bi / ₣Bl three-clone parallelization plan from first principles, compared that independent re-derivation against the installed barriers, and reported a severity-ranked set of findings. Verdict: the plan is **structurally sound** — no silent barrier gap, no dependency cycle, clean referent integrity, all 13 cross-clone writes barrier-covered. The defects were **docket staleness and paddock drift** left by the aggressive LLM restructuring (the big restring + paddock restitch + barrier insertion), not coordination errors. The operator then directed applying every fix; this was done as 12 docket reslates (5 ₣Bf, 4 ₣Bi, 3 ₣Bl) and 3 paddock edits. `jjx_validate` came back clean.

## Why it restored confidence (the method, not the result)
The result alone ("plan is sound") is worth little — a confident agent will say that about anything. What makes the verdict trustworthy is the *shape of the procedure that produced it*, and three properties did the work:

**1. The answer key was withheld.** Each of the 38 remaining non-barrier paces was re-footprinted COLD — files-written and hard-dependencies derived from the docket plus a live grep of the repo — with the installed barrier dockets deliberately kept out of the footprinting agents' inputs. The point is to reconstruct the dependency graph the plan SHOULD have, independently, so it can be diffed against the one the plan DOES have. An audit that reads the barriers first cannot find a missing barrier; it can only nod along.

**2. Every finding was adversarially verified, including the auditor's own guesses.** My first cold read produced candidate findings — and the verification pass refuted two of the scariest ones I had flagged HIGH:
- A suspected *barrier gap before the combinator pace* (it sits at order 1, ahead of the foedus-design barrier, and its docket leaned on that design). Refuted: the combinator lifts already-committed substrate and consumes nothing cross-clone; the docket prose was stale, not a real dependency.
- A suspected *build-breaking modify/delete conflict* where the federation stream retires terrier fixtures that the theurge stream concurrently refactors. Refuted by the merge arithmetic: the zipper-baton barrier folds the theurge helper-extraction into the federation clone's ancestry *before* the deletion, so final convergence is a clean delete, not a conflict.

The discipline that matters: the verification was as skeptical of my conclusions as of the plan's. Findings that survived an honest attempt to kill them are the ones that shipped.

**3. The load-bearing reasoning was merge semantics, made explicit.** Almost every "is this barrier enough?" question reduced to one rule — *a barrier waiting on a late upstream coronet transitively delivers everything earlier in that heat, because commits are linear, and only the waiting heat pulls.* Writing that rule down once and doing the order arithmetic against it (which barrier delivers which coronets, in which clone, before which dependent pace) is what turned vague worry into either a confirmed cover or a precise gap. The completeness critic then walked all 13 cross-clone writes against the rule and found no uncovered edge — and closed the one edge whose arithmetic would have been fatal had it been real (a theurge pace appearing to consume a federation arm that, per its own docket, it explicitly defers).

## The shape of the defects that DID survive
Not coordination errors — **provenance hygiene**. The restructuring moved paces between heats (each getting a new coronet, the old one freed), restitched three paddocks under a heat-gestalt shift, and inserted barriers. The damage was exactly where you'd predict: dockets written against now-demolished code that never self-acknowledged it; a docket "fix" resting on an inverted premise (a live spec called a dangle); barrier prose promising work no pace does; stale ordering claims at the head of a heat; paddock drift (a cross-heat silks name in prose, a phantom drained item with no destination, a count that didn't follow its own list, design decisions attributed to the wrong stream after the move). Each bites a mount agent — it reads true and sends the agent at the wrong target — but none corrupts the cross-clone choreography. That distinction (legibility defect vs coordination hazard) is what let the severities stay honest: no HIGH, a cluster of MEDIUM docket/paddock fixes, a few LOW.

## The reusable playbook
This worked well enough to repeat the next time a heavily-LLM-restructured plan needs auditing:

- **Scout inline, then fan out.** Pull the change ledger, the per-heat inventories (remaining + full, for referent resolution), and every docket + paddock into durable scratch files first. Read them yourself — do not delegate the reading and then synthesize on top of files you never opened (that is a confabulation trap; it was named and avoided here).
- **Workflow A: footprint + self-refute per pace**, answer-key withheld, into durable per-pace files.
- **Inline: assemble the cross-clone graph yourself** and reconcile against the installed barriers. This step is the auditor's, not a subagent's — it is where the merge arithmetic lives.
- **Workflow B: adversarially verify each candidate finding** (show the order arithmetic), cover the dimension the inline pass deferred (here, paddock↔reality, one agent per heat), and run a completeness critic that walks every cross-clone write hunting the one defect class that matters most — a real dependency with no barrier.
- **Synthesize severity-ranked, immediately-actionable findings**, then (on operator direction) apply as reslates.

## Honest residuals and costs
- **Cost.** ~8M subagent tokens across the fan-outs. The first Workflow A launch (16-wide) tripped a server-side rate limit hard; the fix was to make progress durable (each agent writes its result to a file) and cap concurrency by running paces in small sequential chunks. That pattern — durable per-item files + chunked fan-out + re-run only the missing items — is the right default for any large fan-out under a flaky throttle.
- **A latent bug surfaced, not yet dispositioned:** `rbq_qualify`'s colophon-freshness check is a dead no-op — it logs "Checked 0 RBW tabtargets" (a launcher-path-match bug). This means the ₣Bl colophon-relocation pace's tabtarget renames are runtime-load-bearing but NOT qualify-enforced; the reslated docket now says "verify by dispatch, not by qualify." The qualify dead-check itself wants its own pace or itch.
- **One finding was flagged, not resolved:** the federation estate-secret stub verifies against a "committed canonical JWKS" that no pace produces (the producer step was orphaned by two operator decisions). The reslate surfaces the gap in the docket and marks it an operator call (folding the commit into the stub would cross the stub's "source-plus-verify only" cinch) — deliberately not decided by the audit.
- **What the audit cannot certify:** "normalized/valid" is structural, not semantic. The audit verified the *coordination* is sound and the *dockets* now read true; it did not re-decide any design the operator already cinched, and a clean `jjx_validate` means the bytes are canonical, not that the plan is wise.

## The directive deserves credit
The audit worked because the directive (`memo-20260625-three-heat-integrity-audit-directive.md`) specified the method that works: fresh chat, re-derive cold, do not trust prior agents' conclusions, withhold the installed barriers while footprinting, adversarially verify, hunt the barrier-gap class hardest. The procedure was the product. If there is a durable lesson, it is that *for an LLM-restructured plan, an independent cold re-derivation with adversarial self-checking is the cheapest honest confidence instrument we have* — and that its findings cluster in provenance hygiene, where the restructuring's seams show.
